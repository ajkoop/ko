Welcome to Web Design

My goal in this course is to build your skills and motivate you to move beyond this course and carve your own path — whether you become a programmer, content editor, or part-time code dabbler — I want you to leave this course with confidence.

We’ll start the semester by learning a bit about one another. We'll introduce ourselves on August 24th in Ernie Pyle Hall at 1:30pm in Room 214. After a short syllabus quiz (due 11:59pm, Tuesday the 25th), your first assignment (due 11:59pm, Thursday the 27th) is a learning contract that tells the class where you are as a web designer and where you would like to be. It increases our chance of success this semester when we know what to expect from one another. 

If you've never submitted an assignment in Canvas before or if you'd like a refresher on the process, please view my quick Canvas assignment submission tutorial (Links to an external site.).

Required Reading

We will be reading HTML and CSS: Design and Build Websites (Links to an external site.) by Jon Duckett in this course. Please purchase it as soon as possible to ensure that you have enough time to read it. When you first pick it up, you'll say, "Hey, that's heavy." But, don't worry. It's more like a well laid out coffee table book than a dense, heady textbook. I think you'll enjoy reading through it.

Course Rules

  1. Commit to helping one another. I commit to helping you understand the tools you’ll need to write and publish code. I expect you to leave thoughtful feedback to your classmates. You will be assigned two classmates at random to assist with feedback that is grounded in constructive criticism, encouragement, observation, and suggestion. You should know that I am strict when it comes to quality feedback. Be courteous and thorough. Give the kind of feedback that you’d want to receive.
  2. Turn in your assignments on time. We all make mistakes sometimes. You can receive half credit for your first late assignment, but you will not receive credit for subsequent late assignments. I have exceptions for funerals and illnesses, but be sure to scan and email documentation. 
  3. Contact me for help. You might reach a point in this class where you are feeling a bit lost. Feedback from your classmates might clarify things for you and even raise your spirits. However, if you get stuck, never hesitate to contact me via email (koop@iu.edu). I strive to answer all emails within 24 hours.

Professional Expectations

IU Journalism is accredited by the Accrediting Council on Education in Journalism and Mass Communications (ACEJMC), which reviews its program every six years. Receiving accredited status is an indication that the School’s courses for journalism majors are addressing the values and competencies (Links to an external site.) established by the ACEJMC.

The list of twelve values and competencies can generally be applied in total to J360, however the following five are particularly important to achieve for the course:

  • Understand concepts and apply theories in the use and presentation of images and information;
  • Think critically, creatively and independently;
  • Write correctly and clearly in forms and styles appropriate for the communications professions, audiences and purposes they serve;
  • Critically evaluate their own work and that of others for accuracy and fairness, clarity, appropriate style and grammatical correctness;
  • Apply tools and technologies appropriate for the communications professions in which they work.
